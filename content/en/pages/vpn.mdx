There is a lot of debate online about whether a VPN is useful or not.

For activists specifically, our online activity can be used against us in court, even if what we're doing is legal.

A VPN is an app you install on your phone or computer that redirects all your internet traffic through its servers. It's main purpose is to change your IP address. Normally all the devices in your house have the same IP address. If you use a VPN, you share the same IP address with thousands of other users. While this can provide a tiny bit of additional privacy, it's not as big of a privacy win as most VPN marketing webpages and YouTuber sponsored ads would have you believe.

## Busting myths about VPNs

(test)

#### ❌ FALSE: A VPN makes you anonymous online
A VPN only changes your IP address. Most major websites (Google, Instagram, etc.) use a combination of factors to to uniquely identify you. This is called your browser's "fingerprint." It includes things like your browser version, operating system, screen size, timezone, browser plugins, and more. Check out [Cover Your Tracks from EFF](https://coveryourtracks.eff.org/) for an example. 

To be anonymous online, use [Tor Browser](https://www.torproject.org/) for your most sensitive online activity.

#### ❌ FALSE: A VPN means the cops can't identify you
Same answer as above. The cops have the ability to subpoena your internet provider for the domains you have visisted. 

If you choose an untrustworthy VPN that keeps logs, the cops can also subpoena that data.

#### ❌ FALSE: Using a VPN based in another country means the US government can't get the data
The US has a something called a "mutual legal assistance treaty" (MLAT) with many countries. This means they can submit a warrant to that country and they will use their legal system to execute the warrant. It usually does take a lot more work and time than if the company were based in the US, so there is a slight advantage. But it doesn't mean the data is safe.

Even if your VPN doesn't keep logs, the cops could ask them for your payment information if you use a VPN attached to your email address. (This is why we recommend services like Mullvad and IVPN that give you a unique account ID not attached to an email.)

#### ❌ FALSE: You need a VPN when on public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, airports, etc.)
The advice to use a VPN when on public Wi-Fi was popular around 2010-2015. But since then, most websites now use a secure connection (HTTPS) by default. This means that the Wi-Fi network can only see domains you visit (example.com), not the specific page you are (example.com/activist-news-article) on or the content on the page (like your bank account number).

#### 🟠 MOSTLY FALSE: A VPN prevents you from being hacked
By default, a VPN does not prevent the most common kind of attack phishing (where you accidentally give your password to a fake website). 

**However:** Some VPNs do have a setting that block malware sites, but most browsers already have a similar setting available.

#### 🟠 MOSTLY FALSE: A VPN blocks trackers and ads
It's true that many VPNs have a feature to block trackers and ads. But you can get this same protection in your browser by using a privacy-focused browser like [Brave](https://brave.com/)(or [Firefox](https://www.mozilla.org/en-US/firefox/new/) with [uBlock Origin](https://ublockorigin.com/) installed).

#### 🟠 MOSTLY FALSE: Using a VPN is better than nothing at all
A VPN can see all of your internet traffic. If they keep logs, they can turn this data over to the cops just like your internet provider could. They can also use this to sell data about you to advertisers and data brokers. So choosing an untrustworthy VPN is often worse than using nothing at all.

#### 🟠 MOSTLY FALSE: A free VPN is fine
Free VPNs that you can find in the app store or online are most likely selling your data to data brokers to cover their costs.

The only trustworthy free VPNs we can recommend are [Proton VPN](https://proton.me/vpn) and [RiseupVPN](https://riseup.net/en/vpn).

#### 🟠 MOSTLY FALSE: If I pay for a VPN, it's safe
Most VPNs are not trustworthy (see our list below). Just because you're paying for it, doesn't mean it's safe.

#### ✅ You want to make online activity slightly harder to trace 
Many smaller and medium-sized websites don't have fancy fingerprinting technology to uniquely identify you. When you use your normal internet provider, you get an IP address that is unique to the devices in your house. So websites (and the cops) have an easier time determining which internet traffic is connected to you. When you use a VPN, you share the same IP address with thousands of other users.

But as stated above, this is a very small improvement and doesn't mean you're anonymous online. If you need a real guarantee of anonymity, [Tor Browser](https://www.torproject.org/) is the best option.

## Downsides to using a VPN {#downsides}

* You will encounter more CAPTCHAs on websites (those little "prove you are a human" puzzles)
* Some websites may block VPN access, and you'll have to disable it and remember to re-enable it later
* Some streaming services might not work

If you experience odd behavior on a website, try turning off the VPN temporarily to see if it will load. IVPN offers a "pause for 5 minutes" option, which helps you not have to remember to turn it back on later.

*Note:* You must use a trusted VPN that doesn't keep logs of your internet traffic and will push back on government requests. We've vetted our top recommendations.


## ✅ VPNs we recommend for activists {#recommended}
* **[IVPN](https://www.ivpn.net/)**: We recommend IVPN for activists. They've bulit trust over the long term with privacy advocates.
* **[Proton VPN](https://proton.me/vpn)**: Good if you need a free option. (Slight downside: it is harder to have an anonymous account and payment method.)
* **[RiseupVPN](https://riseup.net/en/vpn)**: Good if you need a free option. Doesn't receive frequent security updates, but it is run by trusted activists in the US. (Normally we don't recommend using US-based VPNs, but we trust the people running Riseup to resist a warrant or subpoena as much as they can.)

## 🟡 VPNs that we don't have a verdict on yet {#maybe}
* **[Windscribe](https://www.windscribe.com/)**: Has a decent track record. Not as feature-rich as IVPN.
* **[NymVPN](https://nym.com/)**: Has an interesting design, but too new for us to recommend. Needs a security audit and a longer track record. Possible that their "mixnet" mode might actually be as secure or more secure than Tor, but it will be a while (years) before we know that for sure. Founder appears to have leftist politics.
* **[Obscura](https://obscura.com/)**: Looks legit, but too new for us to recommend. Needs a security audit and a longer track record.
* **[AirVPN](https://airvpn.org/)**: Popular among some privacy advocates.
* **[Hide.me](https://hide.me/)**: Decent track record, and we have no big flags. But it doesn't have the anonymous account features that IVPN offers (through vouchers). And it hasn't invested as much in security audits over the years.
* **[Apple iCloud Private Relay](https://support.apple.com/en-us/102602)**: Built into iCloud+ on iPhones and Macs, so many Apple users already have it on. It's quite well-designed (your traffic goes through not just Apple, but also a 3rd party provider, so it is much harder to track), but it only works on Safari and Apple Mail. So it isn't a full solution.

## ❌ VPNs we do NOT recommend {#avoid}
We strongly suggest **not using any VPN that isn't in the recommended list above**. 

A VPN has access to the domain name (example.com) of every website you visit, and can turn this information over to the cops ([like PureVPN did for the FBI](https://torrentfreak.com/purevpn-explains-how-it-helped-the-fbi-catch-a-cyberstalker-171016/)) or sell it to data brokers, which the cops also sometimes use for warrantless surveillance.
