## What is Tor, and why might I want it?

Tor is a free privacy tool that hides what you do online from most of the people who would otherwise be watching. It's highly trusted by privacy experts around the world.

Without Tor, every site you visit gets logged in a few places. Your internet provider (Comcast, AT&T, T-Mobile, whoever) keeps a record of every site you load, and they have to hand those records over when law enforcement, ICE, or a federal agency legally requests them. Whoever runs the network you're on (your employer, your school, the coffee shop, your landlord's building Wi-Fi) can see the same thing. And the sites themselves see your real IP address, which reveals your approximate location and can be tracked across your web activity to identify you.

With Tor, your traffic bounces through three random volunteer-run computers somewhere in the world before it reaches the site you're going to. Your internet provider can tell you're using Tor, but not what you're reading. The site sees a Tor exit point in another country, not you. Your local Wi-Fi sees only an encrypted blob.

You can use [Tor Browser](https://www.torproject.org/download/) to visit any site with full anonymity. (As long as you don't reveal your identity by providing information to a site or logging into a website attached to your real-world identity.)

### What our onion address adds on top of that

When you read our regular site (ActivistChecklist.org) inside Tor Browser, you're already getting most of the protection above. Your traffic still has to leave Tor through an "exit node" (someone's computer running Tor) to reach our server, and that exit sees the connection (though not who you are).

Loading the onion address above keeps your traffic inside the Tor network the whole way, with no detour out through the regular internet. The address itself is the cryptographic proof that you're on our real site, not a phishing copy.
